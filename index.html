The Challenge

We received a computer vision prototype from Army Research Laboratories that wasn't in great shape‚Äîoutdated model, unmaintained code, documentation that didn't exist. We could have tried to salvage it, but it would have taken longer than starting fresh, so we built a real-time ML inference platform from scratch that could handle 18 concurrent camera streams at 30-40 FPS, keep latency under 70ms, and hit 75%+ accuracy across 13 different target types.

Here's where it got interesting. Six months into development, with RGB cameras fully optimized, the customer mentioned almost in passing, "Oh, and we'll need IR capability too. Soldiers will definitely use that." Infrared. Completely different data stream. Two months until our major evaluation event.

That one hurt, not because it was technically impossible‚Äîwe could do it‚Äîbut because it shouldn't have been a surprise. I should have asked better questions earlier. We delivered RGB + IR in that two-month window, but it taught me something I haven't forgotten. Proactive customer communication isn't optional. You have to check in constantly, surface assumptions early, and not wait for them to remember the critical requirement they forgot to mention.

My Role & Approach

I owned this platform end-to-end‚Äîmodel selection, architecture decisions, delivery schedule. If we missed performance targets or the timeline slipped, that was on me. After getting the project charter approved and assembling the cross-functional team, I ran it from initial prototype through production MVP and still own the roadmap today.

First decision was defining what actually constitutes "good enough" for this system. I defined hard gates at 30-40 FPS processing, sub-70ms latency, and 75%+ accuracy, and those weren't arbitrary. Early versions were detecting tree branches and shadows as drones constantly, which made soldiers want to turn the system off. If the false positive rate is high enough that users don't trust it, the accuracy number doesn't matter‚Äîit's unusable.

We evaluated YOLO model variants (small, medium, large) and selected medium for the best balance between speed and accuracy. The Army Research Laboratories prototype was rejected‚Äîit would have taken longer to fix than building from scratch.

Big architecture call was migrating from Python to C++, because Python wasn't going to meet real-time performance requirements. Then we hit GPU bottlenecks, so I pushed for a client-server refactor to split frame extraction from inference and distribute the load across dual RTX 4090 systems over 100 Gigabit Ethernet. That got us from 3 streams to 18 with about 2√ó the throughput.

The IR situation was a learning moment. Six months in, the customer mentioned needing infrared support with two months to delivery, so I reset expectations with leadership‚Äîthis is happening, but here's what it costs‚Äîand we delivered. Both RGB and IR working in that window. The lesson stuck. I now implement continuous stakeholder alignment checkpoints, because waiting for requirements to surface late is a recipe for disaster.

Technical Solution

Client-Server Architecture Refactor

Replaced single-executable design (which overloaded GPU) with distributed client-server model: clients extract frames, servers perform inference across dual RTX 4090 GPUs per system via 100 Gigabit Ethernet. Enabled scale from 3 streams ‚Üí 18 streams with ~2√ó throughput improvement.

Multi-Camera Tracking Algorithm

Evolved from independent per-camera tracking (objects moving between cameras treated as new detections) to stitched ultrawide FOV approach, treating all detections as single panoramic image. Solved cross-camera object persistence problem.

FP32‚ÜíFP16 Model Optimization

Converted YOLO medium model computations from 32-bit to 16-bit floating point precision, significantly reducing memory footprint and bandwidth requirements without compromising accuracy. Critical for multi-stream GPU efficiency.
